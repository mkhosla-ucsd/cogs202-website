Course Description

This course surveys key methods and practices of computational modeling across a range of cognitive science fields. Bringing together basic tools of machine learning, AI, computational neuroscience, and probabilistic modeling, students will learn to use quantitative modeling to fit data, formulate hypotheses, and explain cognitive phenomena.

Learning Objectives

  • Read, comprehend, and critically evaluate primary research literature in computational cognitive science.
  • Identify different levels and styles of computational models.
  • Compare and contrast alternative computational modeling approaches.
  • Assess the strengths and potential pitfalls of computational models.
  • Design and implement computational modeling projects relevant to your own research questions within the cognitive sciences.

Materials and Course Structure

Each week will focus on a selected topic in computational modeling of cognition, representing a (biased) cross-section of key topics and modeling styles. Readings will include both papers for student presentations and additional readings.

Homework problems will provide practice with the basic concepts. Although students are encouraged to work in groups on homework, each student should write up their own solutions.

Presentations: Each student will present a research article once during the quarter (20 minutes presentation followed by 5 minutes of Q&A). Sign up for your presentation from the list in the tentative lecture schedule below at the following sign-up link.

Final Project: There will be a final project as part of the course requirements.

Final Project Guidelines

COGS 202 Spring 2025

  • Presentation: In class on Week 10, Monday, June 2
  • Team Formation: Work in groups of 2-3 students. A Google Sheet for discussing ideas and forming groups is available here: Google Sheet
  • Proposal: A 1-page proposal is due May 12. The proposal should list the team members, the topic/tentative title, and 1-2 paragraphs about your initial thoughts on how you will approach the project.
  • Final Report: Due on June 6. The report should be roughly 5-8 pages (including references and figures). Your write-up should be organized as a scientific paper and include the following sections: Introduction (with a review of related work), Methods/Models, Results, and Discussion/Conclusion. For example, see:
    Singh, P., Peterson, J. C., Battleday, R. M., & Griffiths, T. L. (2020). End-to-end deep prototype and exemplar models for predicting human behavior. Proceedings of the 42nd Annual Conference of the Cognitive Science Society. [link here]
  • Computational Work: The project must include some computational work (e.g., simulations, calculations, or computational analysis of experimental data).
  • Deliverable: A key deliverable is a set of specific, concrete predictions for the results of a proposed experimental test of the theory.
  • Project Format Options:
    • Develop a computational model or analysis using your own research data.
    • Reproduce and extend a result from one of the research papers we read in class.
    • Develop a grant proposal for an experimental or computational project, including preliminary simulation or computational analysis validating the proposed idea.

Course Policies

Attendance

This course is discussion-based, so attendance is essential for both benefiting from and contributing to class. While attendance is not directly graded, participation is. We understand that conferences and other events may lead to absences; please communicate any planned absences in advance. A limited number of absences will be allowed.

Collaboration

Discussion of class material is heavily encouraged. Collaboration on homework assignments is allowed as long as it is properly reported. Project collaboration is expected (projects are done in groups) and is also allowed across groups.

Academic Integrity

There is a zero tolerance policy for violations of academic integrity and course policies. If you have any doubts regarding the policies, please clarify with the course staff before proceeding.

Late Homework Policy

The maximum earnable points for each assignment will drop by 20% for each day an assignment is late.
