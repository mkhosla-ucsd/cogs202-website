Course Description

This course surveys key methods and practices of computational modeling across a range of cognitive science fields. Bringing together basic tools of machine learning, AI, computational neuroscience, and probabilistic modeling, students will learn to use quantitative modeling to fit data, formulate hypotheses, and explain cognitive phenomena.

Learning Objectives

  • Read, comprehend, and critically evaluate primary research literature in computational cognitive science.
  • Identify different levels and styles of computational models.
  • Compare and contrast alternative computational modeling approaches.
  • Assess the strengths and potential pitfalls of computational models.
  • Design and implement computational modeling projects relevant to your own research questions within the cognitive sciences.

Materials and Course Structure

Each week will focus on a selected topic in computational modeling of cognition, representing a (biased) cross-section of key topics and modeling styles. Readings include 3-4 classic or contemporary research articles (or review articles) related to the topic.

Homework problems will give you practice with the basic concepts. Although students are encouraged to work in groups, each student should write up their own solutions. There will also be a final project to be completed in teams.

Grading Breakdown:

  • Attendance and participation: 5%
  • Homework (3 problem sets, 15% each): 45% total
  • Paper presentation: 20%
  • Final project: 30%

Final Project Guidelines

  • Presentation: In class on Week 10, Friday, June 8
  • Team Formation: Work in groups of 2-3 students. (Google Sheet for project ideas and group formation)
  • Proposal: A 1-page proposal is due May 18. It should list team members, the topic/tentative title, and 1-2 paragraphs outlining your initial project approach.
  • Final Report: Due together with your presentation on June 8. The report should be roughly 5-10 pages (including references and figures).
  • Project Requirements: The project must include some computational work (e.g., simulations, calculations, or computational analysis of experimental data) and deliver specific, concrete predictions for an experimental test of the theory.
  • Format Options:
    • Develop a computational model or analysis using your own research data.
    • Reproduce and extend a result from one of the research papers discussed in class.
    • Develop a grant proposal including preliminary simulation or computational analysis.
